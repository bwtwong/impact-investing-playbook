Core Distinction
ESG vs Impact Investing
Avoiding harm is not the same as creating good. The spectrum every investor must place themselves on.
SpectrumIntentionalityAdditionality

The capital allocation spectrum

  • Traditional investing: Financial return only, no consideration of impact
  • ESG integration: Considers environmental, social, governance factors as financial risk factors — primarily about risk management, not impact creation
  • Responsible/Negative screening: Excludes harmful sectors (tobacco, weapons, fossil fuels) — avoids harm but doesn't actively create good
  • Sustainable/Thematic investing: Actively invests in sustainability themes (clean energy, etc.) but impact may not be the primary intent
  • Impact investing: Intentional, measurable positive social/environmental impact alongside financial return — impact is a core objective, not a side effect
  • Philanthropy / Grants: Impact-only, no expectation of financial return

The three defining features of true impact investing

  1. Intentionality: The investor's intention to generate positive social/environmental impact through investments is essential
  2. Measurement: A commitment to measure and report the social and environmental performance of investments
  3. Additionality: The investment makes a difference beyond what would have happened anyway (without it, this outcome wouldn't occur, or wouldn't occur at this scale/speed)

Where "impact washing" happens

  • Labelling ESG-integrated funds as "impact" when they only manage risk, not create outcomes
  • Investing in a company that's "good" but where the investment itself adds no additionality (e.g. buying public equity in an already well-funded company)
  • Claiming credit for impact metrics the company would have achieved regardless of the investment

Why the distinction matters practically

  • Affects what metrics you track, how you structure deals, and what return expectations are appropriate
  • LPs (limited partners) increasingly demand clarity on where a fund sits — vague positioning damages credibility and fundraising
  • Different asset classes lend themselves to different points on the spectrum — private equity/venture has higher additionality potential than public equities

Investment Strategy
Developing an Impact Thesis
The single document that defines what an investor believes about how capital creates change — and where they'll deploy it.
StrategyMandateFund Design

What an impact thesis defines

  • Impact goal: The specific social/environmental outcome being pursued (often linked to SDGs)
  • Target population/geography: Who benefits, and where
  • Theory of change: How capital deployment leads to that outcome
  • Asset class and stage: Equity, debt, blended; seed to growth to mature
  • Return expectations: Market-rate, below-market, or capital preservation
  • Risk appetite: What level of impact and financial risk is acceptable

Building the thesis

  1. Start with the impact problem, not the financial product — what change are you trying to create?
  2. Map the capital gap — where is funding genuinely scarce relative to need? (additionality starts here)
  3. Define your edge — why is your capital, expertise, or network the right fit for this gap?
  4. Set explicit return and impact targets — both should be falsifiable, not aspirational platitudes
  5. Pressure-test against alternative uses of the same capital — would another allocation create more impact or return?

The return-impact tension

Most impact theses must take an explicit position on the relationship between financial return and impact: are they correlated (impact drives financial outperformance), in tension (some impact requires return concession), or genuinely uncorrelated? Vague answers here undermine investor trust.

Common thesis weaknesses

  • Too broad — "we invest in companies doing good" isn't a thesis, it's a mood
  • No clear theory of how the specific capital deployed causes the specific outcome
  • Confusing the fund's impact thesis with individual portfolio companies' theories of change
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: You're designing an impact thesis for a new $50M fund focused on improving financial inclusion for First Nations communities in regional Australia. Draft the core elements: impact goal, target population, theory of change (2-3 sentences), asset class/stage focus, and return expectations. Take an explicit position on the return-impact relationship.

Investment Logic
Theory of Change (Investor Lens)
Not just a grantmaker's tool. For an investor, the Theory of Change is the causal spine underneath every impact thesis — and the thing due diligence is actually testing.
Causal ChainAssumptionsDue Diligence Input

Why investors need this distinctly from programme designers

A nonprofit's Theory of Change explains how a programme creates change. An investor's Theory of Change has an extra link the programme version doesn't need: it must also explain how the capital itself — not just the activities it funds — contributes to that change. This is the difference between "this programme helps people" and "this specific investment helps this programme help people, more than it otherwise would."

The investor-specific causal chain

  • Capital input: What does this specific cheque enable that wouldn't otherwise happen? (the additionality link)
  • Enterprise activity: What the investee actually does with the capital
  • Outcome: The change experienced by the target population
  • Investor contribution: Separating the enterprise's theory of change from the investor's own — capital, governance, network, signalling

Where this shows up across the investment lifecycle

  1. At thesis stage: the fund's overall Theory of Change sets out why this asset class/sector/geography is the right place to deploy capital for impact
  2. At due diligence: the enterprise's Theory of Change is tested for logical coherence — does each link actually follow from the one before it?
  3. At monitoring: leading indicators are chosen specifically because they sit early in the causal chain, giving warning before lagging outcome data arrives
  4. At exit: a sale that breaks the causal chain (e.g. to an acquirer who'll abandon the activity that drove the outcome) is a Theory of Change failure, not just a financial event

How this differs from the consulting-sector treatment

The Consulting Playbook's Theory of Change entry is written from a programme design and evaluation lens — useful for NGOs and government services building and assessing their own programmes. This entry is written from the capital allocator's seat: the same underlying logic, but with the investor's own contribution made explicit as a distinct, testable link in the chain.

Core Concept
Additionality
Would this have happened anyway? The single hardest, most important question in impact investing.
CounterfactualCausationIFC

The four types of additionality

  • Financial additionality: The capital wouldn't have been available from other sources (market gap)
  • Value-add additionality: The investor provides more than capital — expertise, networks, governance that improves outcomes
  • Signalling additionality: The investment signals credibility that crowds in other capital
  • Demonstration additionality: Proves a model works, enabling replication elsewhere

Why it's hard to prove

True additionality requires knowing the counterfactual — what would have happened without this investment. Since you can't run the experiment twice, additionality is always an estimate, supported by reasoning (would another investor have funded this at the same terms and speed?) rather than certainty.

Practical assessment approach

  1. Ask: is this a capital-constrained market, or would commercial capital have stepped in anyway?
  2. Assess deal terms — is the investor accepting below-market terms (concessionary capital) that signal genuine market gap-filling?
  3. Examine timing — would this company/project have happened later, smaller, or not at all without this capital?
  4. Document the counterfactual reasoning explicitly, even if imperfect — silence on additionality is worse than an honest, imperfect estimate

The IFC's "but for" test

  • The International Finance Corporation uses a "but for" test: but for this investment, would the project have happened at all, at this scale, with this quality, or this speed?
  • Strongest additionality claims combine financial AND value-add additionality — capital plus genuine expertise/governance improvement
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Scenario: An impact fund is considering a $5M investment in a profitable, well-established social enterprise that already has strong revenue and could likely raise capital from mainstream commercial investors at similar terms. Assess the additionality case for this investment using the "but for" test, and identify what would need to be true for this to be a genuinely additional investment.

Global Standard
Five Dimensions of Impact (IMP)
What, Who, How Much, Contribution, Risk. The shared language for structuring any impact claim, from the Impact Management Project.
IMP NormsIndustry StandardStructure

The five dimensions

WhatThe outcome, whether positive/negative, and how important it is to those experiencing it
WhoThe stakeholders experiencing the outcome, and how underserved they are relative to the outcome
How MuchScale (how many), depth (degree of change), duration (how long it lasts)
ContributionWhether the outcome is better than what would likely have occurred otherwise (additionality)
RiskThe likelihood that impact will differ from what's expected

Background

Developed through the Impact Management Project (2016–2021), a consensus-building effort across 2,000+ organisations. Since 2021, stewardship passed to Impact Frontiers. It's now the most widely referenced standard in impact investing — embedded in IRIS+, the IFC's Operating Principles for Impact Management, and most investor frameworks.

How investors apply it

  1. For each investment, profile the outcome across all five dimensions using a structured rubric (often 0–3 or 1–4 scoring)
  2. "How Much" should always be quantified using specific metrics; "What," "Who," and "Risk" combine qualitative and quantitative evidence
  3. "Contribution" should be supported by logically argued, ideally empirically tested, counterfactual reasoning
  4. Investors add a sixth implicit dimension: their own contribution (was the capital itself additional, distinct from the enterprise's contribution)

IMP vs IRIS+: the key distinction

  • The Five Dimensions tell you how to structure an impact claim (the question architecture)
  • IRIS+ tells you what to measure (the indicator catalogue — 500+ standardised metrics across sectors)
  • Most practitioners use both together: profile outcomes using the Five Dimensions, then populate metrics from IRIS+

Indicator Catalogue
IRIS+
500+ standardised metrics across 17 sectors. The GIIN's answer to "what exactly should I measure?"
GIINMetricsComparability

What it provides

  • Standardised metrics across 17 sectors (agriculture, education, energy, financial inclusion, health, housing, etc.)
  • "Core Metrics Sets" — curated subsets of the most decision-useful indicators per theme, reducing the overwhelm of the full catalogue
  • Direct alignment to the Five Dimensions of Impact (IMP) and SDG targets
  • Evidence briefings explaining why specific metrics matter for specific outcomes

The selection discipline

While IRIS+ offers 500+ metrics, most programmes need only 12–18 core metrics. Measuring everything creates measurement fatigue and dilutes focus. Selection should follow directly from the impact thesis — measure what validates your specific theory of change, not everything that's available.

Implementation approach

  1. State the impact thesis explicitly before selecting metrics
  2. Identify the outcome the thesis predicts
  3. Select IRIS+ Core Metrics that directly validate that outcome
  4. Add a small number of context metrics that explain variance (e.g. demographic breakdowns)
  5. Resist the urge to add metrics "just in case" — each metric has a real data collection cost

The comparability value

  • Standardised metrics enable portfolio-level aggregation — comparing impact across very different investments
  • Enables co-investment and syndication — multiple investors can use the same data dictionary
  • Critical for LP reporting where capital comes from multiple sources with different reporting needs

Select 4-5 IRIS+-style metrics (you don't need exact IRIS+ codes — describe the metrics) that would directly validate this specific thesis, distinguishing core metrics from context metrics.

Risk Management
Impact Risk Assessment
Financial risk has a whole discipline. Impact risk needs its own — the ways expected outcomes fail to materialise.
Risk CategoriesMitigationIMP Risk

The impact risk categories

  • Evidence risk: The assumption that the activity creates the outcome is wrong or untested
  • Execution risk: The enterprise fails to deliver the activities as planned
  • External risk: Outside factors (economic shocks, policy changes) disrupt the outcome
  • Stakeholder participation risk: Intended beneficiaries don't engage or experience the outcome differently than expected
  • Durability / drop-off risk: The outcome doesn't persist over time
  • Unexpected impact risk: Negative or unintended consequences occur alongside the intended positive impact

Risk scoring approach

Practitioners typically rate each risk category by likelihood × consequence (similar to financial risk matrices), producing an overall impact risk rating per investment. This should happen at due diligence (ex ante) and be revisited through monitoring (ex post).

Managing impact risk

  1. Design stage: build measurement plans and mitigation strategies into the investment structure from day one
  2. Due diligence: explicitly score each risk category, don't just assess financial risk
  3. Monitoring: track leading indicators of risk materialising (e.g. declining engagement = stakeholder participation risk emerging)
  4. Reporting: be transparent ex post about which risks materialised and how they were managed

Why this matters for return

  • Impact risk and financial risk are often correlated — an enterprise failing to deliver impact is frequently also failing operationally
  • For impact-linked financial instruments (e.g. outcomes-based contracts), impact risk directly determines financial return

Deal Structuring Deal Structuring for Impact
›
Capital Structure
Blended Finance
Using concessional public/philanthropic capital to de-risk and mobilise larger pools of commercial investment.
Catalytic CapitalTranchesSIFI

What it is

Blended finance is a structuring approach that uses capital from philanthropic or public sources to catalyse commercial investment, increasing the total pool of capital available for development outcomes. It's not a single instrument — it's an approach to combining different capital types with different risk/return expectations in the same structure.

Common structures

  • First-loss tranche: Concessional capital absorbs initial losses, protecting commercial investors and reducing their effective risk
  • Guarantees: Public/philanthropic capital guarantees a portion of commercial losses without committing capital upfront
  • Technical assistance facilities: Grant funding pairs with investment capital to build investee capacity, improving investment success odds
  • Results-based / outcomes payments: Returns to certain capital providers depend on achieving pre-defined impact outcomes

Why it works

  1. Commercial investors get risk-adjusted returns close to market rate, despite investing in higher-risk-perceived markets/sectors
  2. Public/philanthropic capital achieves greater leverage — a small amount of concessional capital can unlock much larger commercial pools
  3. The structure makes explicit who bears which risk, rather than asking all capital to accept the same risk-return profile

Risks and criticisms

  • Can subsidise deals that would have attracted commercial capital anyway — undermines additionality
  • Complexity of structuring can consume a significant share of total capital in transaction costs
  • Risk that concessional capital props up structurally unviable projects rather than genuinely catalysing scale
Practice Mode

Apply the framework

Outcomes-Based Finance
Social Impact Bonds (SIBs)
Government pays only if outcomes are achieved. Investors fund the program upfront and bear the performance risk.
Pay-for-SuccessGovernmentOutcomes

How a SIB works

  1. Government/commissioner identifies a social outcome it wants to fund (e.g. reduced recidivism, reduced homelessness)
  2. Private/impact investors provide upfront capital to a service provider to deliver the intervention
  3. An independent evaluator measures whether the agreed outcomes were achieved
  4. Government repays investors (with return) only if outcomes are achieved — the more successful, the higher the return
  5. If outcomes fail, investors lose some or all of their capital — performance risk is transferred from government to investor

The structural logic

SIBs align financial return directly with impact achievement — investors are only paid for genuine outcomes, not just for delivering activities. This shifts government procurement from paying for inputs/outputs to paying for outcomes, and transfers implementation risk to investors who are motivated to ensure delivery quality.

Key design elements

  • Outcome metric: Must be clearly defined, measurable, and attributable to the intervention
  • Independent evaluation: Critical for credibility — evaluator must be free of conflicts of interest
  • Payment schedule: Often tiered — partial payment for partial success, bonus for exceeding targets
  • Counterfactual/comparison group: Needed to attribute outcomes to the intervention rather than general trends

Criticisms and limitations

  • High transaction costs relative to capital deployed — SIBs are expensive and slow to structure
  • Risk of "cherry-picking" easier-to-help beneficiaries to hit outcome targets, leaving harder cases underserved
  • Outcome metrics can be gamed or may not capture the full value of the intervention
  • Government must have a sophisticated enough commissioning capability to design and oversee SIBs well

Instrument Design
Capital Stack & Instrument Selection
Equity isn't always right for impact. Matching the financial instrument to the enterprise's actual growth profile and mission.
EquityDebtRevenue-Based

The instrument options

  • Equity: Highest risk/return, full upside participation, but dilutes founder/mission control — best for high-growth, scalable models
  • Debt: Lower risk, fixed return, doesn't dilute ownership, but requires predictable cash flow to service — suits established revenue models
  • Quasi-equity / mezzanine: Hybrid instruments (convertible notes, revenue participation rights) — flexible, often used when traditional equity or debt don't fit
  • Revenue-based financing: Repayment as a percentage of revenue — aligns investor return with actual performance, useful for cash-flow-positive but non-traditional growth businesses
  • Grants / recoverable grants: No expectation of market return — appropriate for very early-stage or deeply mission-locked models

Matching instrument to enterprise type

Many social enterprises have steady, modest cash flows rather than the hockey-stick growth venture equity expects. Forcing equity-style instruments onto these models creates misaligned incentives (pressure to grow fast at the expense of mission) or simply doesn't fit their economics. The instrument should follow the business model, not the other way around.

Mission protection mechanisms

  1. Golden shares or special voting rights that protect mission-critical decisions regardless of ownership dilution
  2. Mission lock provisions in legal structure (e.g. steward-ownership, B-corp certification requirements written into governance)
  3. Impact-linked terms — interest rates or equity terms that adjust based on impact performance, not just financial performance
  4. Board composition requirements ensuring mission-aligned representation regardless of cap table

Practical decision framework

  • Is the growth trajectory scalable and venture-like, or steady and cash-generative? → equity vs debt/revenue-based
  • How important is founder/mission control preservation? → consider quasi-equity or mission-lock mechanisms
  • What stage is the enterprise at? → grants/recoverable grants for very early stage, equity/debt as it matures

Fund Strategy
Impact Portfolio Construction
Diversification vs concentration, financial risk vs impact risk — building a portfolio that delivers on both mandates.
DiversificationFund DesignRisk Balance

The dual mandate complexity

Traditional portfolio construction optimises for risk-adjusted financial return. Impact portfolios must simultaneously manage a second, often imperfectly correlated objective: aggregate impact achievement. A portfolio optimised purely for return may underweight high-impact, higher-risk investments; one optimised purely for impact may take on excessive financial risk.

Diversification dimensions for impact portfolios

  • Financial risk: Stage, sector, geography (standard portfolio theory)
  • Impact theme: Spreading across multiple impact areas vs concentrating in one theme for deeper expertise and influence
  • Impact risk profile: Balancing higher-evidence, lower-impact-risk investments against unproven, higher-potential models
  • Return expectation: Blending market-rate and below-market (concessionary) investments within the same portfolio if the mandate allows

Construction approach

  1. Define portfolio-level impact and return targets first — these should flow from the fund's impact thesis
  2. Set position sizing rules that account for both financial and impact risk, not just financial risk alone
  3. Decide on thematic concentration vs diversification based on the fund's stated expertise and theory of change
  4. Build a barbell if appropriate: a core of lower-risk, evidence-backed investments plus a smaller allocation to higher-risk, potentially higher-impact bets
  5. Stress-test the portfolio against both financial downside scenarios and impact underperformance scenarios

Common portfolio construction errors

  • Treating impact and financial risk as independent when they're often correlated
  • Over-diversifying across impact themes, diluting expertise and making meaningful impact measurement harder
  • Failing to set explicit rules for how impact underperformance affects future allocation decisions within the fund

Governance Standard
Operating Principles for Impact Management (OPIM)
The IFC-developed standard that defines what it actually means to manage a fund "for impact" across its full lifecycle.
IFCFund GovernanceSignatory Standard

What OPIM is

Developed by the IFC in 2019 with industry consultation, OPIM is a set of principles that funds and institutions can become signatories to, establishing a common discipline for managing investments for impact across the full investment lifecycle — strategy, origination, structuring, portfolio management, exit, and independent verification.

The core principle areas

  • Strategic intent: Define strategic impact objective(s) consistent with the investment strategy
  • Portfolio management: Manage the investment portfolio with reference to the achievement of impact, not just financial return
  • Pre-investment assessment: Assess expected impact at the screening/structuring stage, using a systematic approach
  • Monitoring: Monitor the progress of each investment against expected impact through investment holding period
  • Exit: Conduct exits considering the effect on sustained impact
  • Independent verification: Publicly disclose alignment with the Principles and obtain independent verification

Why signatory status matters

  1. Signals credibility to LPs concerned about impact washing
  2. Requires genuine accountability — independent verification means claims can be checked
  3. Increasingly expected by major institutional LPs (DFIs, pension funds) as a baseline credibility marker
  4. Creates internal discipline — forces a fund to build impact management into operations, not bolt it on as marketing

Practical implementation

  • Requires building impact assessment into deal screening, not just post-investment reporting
  • Exit decisions must explicitly consider impact continuity — a financially attractive exit that destroys impact (e.g. selling to a less mission-aligned acquirer) requires careful navigation
  • Independent verification typically occurs every 2 years, adding both credibility and operational overhead

Impact Due Diligence Process
Financial DD has decades of standard practice. Impact DD is younger — here's the structured approach that's emerged.
ScreeningAssessmentProcess

The impact DD process stages

  1. Impact screening: Does this investment align with the fund's impact thesis at all? (fast filter, eliminates obvious mismatches)
  2. Theory of change validation: Is the enterprise's theory of change logically sound and evidence-based?
  3. Impact measurement system assessment: Does the enterprise already track meaningful impact data, or would this need building?
  4. Five Dimensions profiling: Structured assessment of What/Who/How Much/Contribution/Risk
  5. Additionality assessment: Apply the "but for" test — would this happen without the investment?
  6. Unintended consequence scan: What negative or unexpected impacts might arise?
  7. Impact risk scoring: Rate across the standard risk categories (evidence, execution, external, etc.)
  8. Impact terms negotiation: Build measurement requirements, reporting cadence, and impact covenants into deal terms

Common impact DD failures

  • Treating impact DD as a checkbox exercise run in parallel by a junior team member, disconnected from the core investment decision
  • Accepting enterprise-provided impact claims without independent verification
  • Skipping the unintended consequence scan — most impact failures come from unconsidered negative externalities, not from failing the intended outcome
  • No clear process for what happens if impact DD reveals concerns — does it actually affect the investment decision, or just get noted?

Integrating with financial DD

  • Impact and financial DD should happen in parallel, with findings cross-referenced — operational red flags often show up in both
  • Impact DD findings should explicitly feed into valuation and deal terms, not exist as a separate report nobody reads
  • The investment committee memo should integrate impact findings into the core recommendation, not append them as a separate section

Resourcing impact DD properly

  • Requires genuine sector/impact expertise — not just generalist financial analysts asked to "also check the impact box"
  • Site visits and direct beneficiary engagement (where feasible) provide signal that documents alone cannot

Risk Screening
Impact-Washing Red Flags
A practical checklist for spotting impact claims that won't survive scrutiny — before you commit capital or reputation.
CredibilityScreeningReputation Risk

The classic red flags

  • Vague impact language: "Making the world better" with no specific, measurable outcome defined
  • No baseline data: Impact claims with no "before" state to compare against — change can't be demonstrated without one
  • Output-outcome confusion: Claiming impact based on activity counts (people reached) rather than actual outcome change
  • Cherry-picked metrics: Reporting only favourable indicators while ignoring metrics that would show a fuller picture
  • No counterfactual reasoning: No attempt to address whether the outcome would have happened anyway
  • Impact claims disconnected from business model: The core revenue-generating activity doesn't actually drive the claimed impact

Structural red flags in a fund/enterprise

  1. No dedicated impact measurement resourcing — impact is "everyone's job," meaning effectively no one's
  2. Impact reporting that's inconsistent year to year, making it impossible to track genuine trends
  3. Impact metrics that conveniently always show improvement, with no honest reporting of underperformance
  4. Marketing materials with much stronger impact claims than internal reporting or due diligence documents support

The "so what" test

For any impact claim, ask: if this metric improved, would anyone's life actually be meaningfully better? "10,000 app downloads" fails this test on its own — downloads aren't outcomes. "10,000 people who completed the program reported improved financial literacy scores, sustained at 6-month follow-up" passes.

Due diligence questions to ask

  • "Walk me through exactly how this business activity creates this specific outcome" — vague or circular answers are a red flag
  • "What would happen to your impact metrics if we removed funding tomorrow?" — tests genuine additionality understanding
  • "What's your worst-performing impact metric, and why?" — tests honesty and measurement maturity
